The honest first observation is that the program as it stands rests on an *analogy* — claim is a Proponent design, challenge is an Opponent design, agreement is convergence, disagreement is divergence — and the entire research frontier opens once you try to turn that analogy into a theorem. Almost everything serious follows from taking the Ludics–argumentation correspondence and demanding that it be proved rather than asserted. Let me lay out where I think the real work goes, roughly in order of foundational priority.

The keystone result the whole program needs is a faithful correspondence between Dung-style argument acceptability and Ludics interaction. Right now the platform runs ASPIC+ underneath (defeasible rules, attack, defeat) and a Ludics interaction layer on top, and they're glued by intuition. The foundational theorem would be a soundness-and-completeness bridge: an argument is in the grounded (or preferred, or stable) extension of the abstract argumentation framework *if and only if* its corresponding design is orthogonal to every opponent design in the relevant behaviour. This is genuinely hard, and it's hard for a specific reason worth naming — Dung acceptability is a global fixpoint over an attack graph, a non-interactive property of the whole framework, whereas Ludics convergence is a local property of two designs interacting. The bridge already half-exists in the literature with: Modgil and Caminada's *argument games* give a dialogical, move-by-move characterization of grounded and preferred semantics, and Ludics is precisely a theory of dialogical interaction. So the tractable path is to show that the argument-game characterization of Dung semantics *is* the Ludics dispute, up to a translation that preserves winning strategies. If that goes through, the platform's core claim stops being marketing and becomes a corollary of proof theory. If it doesn't go through cleanly — and it might not, because the preferred semantics involves a maximality condition that has no obvious interactive counterpart — then the interesting result is the precise obstruction, the characterization of *which* argumentation semantics are interactively realizable and which are not. Either outcome is a real paper.

A full-abstraction result: a translation from argumentation structures (ASPIC+ arguments, or AIF graphs) into Ludics designs that is sound and complete with respect to acceptability — a claim is acceptable in the argumentation semantics if and only if its design is winning, i.e. orthogonal to the relevant counter-designs. The technical heart is the conjecture that the acceptable arguments for a claim form a Ludics behaviour — a set of designs closed under bi-orthogonality, B = B^⊥⊥. If you can show that Dung's grounded, preferred, and stable extensions correspond exactly to bi-orthogonally-closed sets of designs, you've shown that two theories of "what survives challenge," developed independently by Girard from proof theory and Dung from nonmonotonic reasoning, coincide — and, more valuably, you've characterized precisely where they diverge, because they almost certainly don't coincide everywhere, and the divergence is where the interesting structure lives. Girard's notion of internal completeness (a behaviour already contains every design you need without appeal to the ambient logic) becomes the formal version of a real design question: whether a deliberation is "closed" — whether every relevant argument is reachable from inside it — or whether it constitutively requires import from other rooms. That last point is not academic; it's the mathematical content of the Plexus.

The second direction is the one I think is the program's deepest opportunity: a categorical, and specifically sheaf-theoretic, semantics of distributed deliberation. The platform already has the structure primed for it — the Plexus network with "transport across deliberations" and "cross-room functors" is functoriality stated in plain English. Ludics has known categorical models (Faggian and Hyland's work on designs as a category, the *-autonomous structure), so the objects-are-issues, morphisms-are-arguments, duality-is-the-proponent/opponent-flip picture is within reach. But the move that would matter is to treat the network of local deliberations as a *site* and the agreements reached in each room as a *presheaf* over it, and then ask the gluing question: when do locally-reached agreements glue into a global one? The answer is that they don't, in general — and the obstruction to gluing is exactly the formal measure of irreducible, structural disagreement. This is the same mathematics Abramsky uses for quantum contextuality (local consistency, global inconsistency, the obstruction living in a cohomology group), transplanted to collective reasoning. A cohomological invariant that quantifies "this disagreement cannot be resolved by any redistribution of local agreements, it is a genuine global obstruction" would be the single most striking theoretical export the platform could produce, and it would matter far beyond the platform. It also vindicates the platform's whole stance: the point was never to force consensus but to locate disagreement precisely, and "the disagreement is a non-trivial cohomology class" is the exact formal content of "this is a real disagreement, not a misunderstanding."

The Plexus is a 2-category — deliberations as objects, transport functors as 1-morphisms, natural transformations left as explicit future work — and transport is restricted to one hop "by contract" because he won't fake functor composition he can't guarantee. The principled lift is a coherence theorem for the Plexus bicategory characterizing exactly when transport composes A→B→C without semantic drift. But the deeper and riskier direction is sheaf-theoretic: model the Plexus as a site — a category whose covering families are the transport relationships — and the local argument structures as a sheaf (a stack, really, since it's a 2-category) over that site. Then global acceptability is the existence of a global section: the local acceptances glue. And the obstruction to gluing — the cohomology of the sheaf — becomes a measure of irreducible, non-local disagreement: not "these rooms disagree" but the topological character of why the disagreement cannot be resolved locally. This is not a fantasy direction; it's the exact structure Abramsky used to render quantum contextuality as an obstruction to global sections, since ported to databases and distributed inference, and Isonomia's cross-room architecture is a natural home for it.

Third, the locus-of-disagreement capability — the "gap identification" the platform sells as its distinctive feature — should become a normalization and separation theorem rather than a heuristic. In Ludics, when two designs fail to be orthogonal, the dispute diverges at a determinate address: the first chronicle position where the two strategies stop matching. The theorem to pursue is that this divergence locus is unique, computable, and corresponds to a *minimal unshared commitment* — a presupposition one party holds and the other challenges or lacks. Girard's separation theorem (designs are distinguished by their behaviour under interaction, the Ludics analogue of Böhm's theorem) is the engine here. If you can show the divergence locus is the minimal separating context, then "the platform identifies exactly where you disagree" is not an engineering claim about a feature, it's a consequence of separation. This is the most directly achievable of the major results because the Ludics machinery for it already exists; the work is the translation and the uniqueness/minimality argument.

Fourth, the quantitative layer needs to be rebuilt. The Dempster-Shafer evidence aggregation is the theoretically weakest component — DS combination is famously ill-behaved (Zadeh's counterexample, the normalization pathologies, non-associativity), and no logician would let it stand. The principled replacement is a *semiring-graded Ludics*: designs carry weights in a commutative semiring, and interaction computes a semiring-valued convergence degree. Pick the Boolean semiring and you recover classical "does it converge"; pick the probability monad or the tropical semiring and you get graded confidence, with the equity metrics falling out as semiring computations over the interaction rather than being bolted on. This rides existing work on quantitative semantics of linear logic (weighted relational models, the Laird–Manzonetto–McCusker–Pagani line) and would unify the qualitative and quantitative into one algebraic object. The deep payoff is that this collapses the distinction between *proof* and *argument*: a mathematical proof is just the Boolean-graded fragment of a defeasible deliberation, an argument that happens to converge with weight 1 against every opponent. Classical proof becomes a special case of graded collective reasoning. Collaborative mathematical proof is multi-party Ludics interaction at Boolean grade, ordinary deliberation is the same interaction at a softer grade.

Getting the confidence algebra right, and this is the most tractable place to get a clean, genuinely new result. The three confidence modes (min, product, Dempster-Shafer) are currently implemented side by side without a unifying theory. The natural move is to recognize that the requirements he already imposes — composition is submultiplicative (c(g∘f) ≥ c(g)•c(f)), join is a semilattice homomorphism, identity is top — are close to the axioms of a quantale: a complete lattice with an associative product that distributes over arbitrary joins, which is exactly the algebra of resources that combine multiplicatively and aggregate by supremum. The conjecture worth proving is that confidence measures should be quantale-valued, with min mode, product mode, and a DS-style structure as three instances, and — the unifying result — that an Isonomia deliberation is a category enriched over the confidence quantale, in the precise sense in which Lawvere showed metric spaces are categories enriched over [0,∞]. The hom-sets don't just carry confidence; the enrichment forces composition to respect the quantale multiplication, which is the submultiplicativity, derived rather than imposed. That single reframing absorbs the Ambler categorical layer and the confidence layer into one structure. The hard part is Dempster-Shafer, because Dempster's combination rule misbehaves under high conflict (the Zadeh counterexample), and a research program would have to either prove the misbehavior is neutralized by the fact that conflict in argumentation is explicitly represented as attacks rather than hidden in the mass assignment, or replace the rule with a quantale-valued or imprecise-probability operation and prove its monotonicity and continuity. The proof-theoretic crown of this thrust would be a graded substructural logic — a refinement of linear logic with a confidence-graded exponential modality !_r, in the lineage of bounded linear logic and the current graded-modal-type work — that is sound and complete for the deliberation semantics, with a cut-elimination theorem. Cut-elimination would be the formal content of resolving a deliberation: normalizing the argument network is eliminating the cuts, and the normal form is the settled state. That is a major but well-posed undertaking, and it's the result that would make the system a logic and not just a data structure.

Fifth, the proof-assistant work is both the most immediately tractable and the most underrated. They've already machine-checked individual conjectures in Agda (the T002 antichain result, the C004 saturation-closure result), which means they know the discipline. The next level is a full formalization of the Ludics core — associativity of interaction, the separation theorem, internal completeness — in a dependent type theory, which essentially does not exist in the literature. That's a contribution to the proof-theory community entirely independent of the platform; people have wanted a mechanized Ludics for twenty years. Alongside it sits a type-theoretic treatment of the Walton schemes: a scheme is a typed inference rule whose critical questions are its elimination obligations, an argument instancing a scheme is a term of the corresponding dependent type, and the question the program already confronted in resolving the C006/C007/C008 trilemma — *when are two schemes identical?* — becomes a univalence-flavored question, schemes are equal exactly when they're behaviourally equivalent. The resolution “scheme identity is behaviour-extensional," is the inferentialist's version of "equivalence is equality," and making that precise in a HoTT setting would tie the argumentation layer to the foundations of mathematics in a non-gratuitous way.

Underneath all of it sits the philosophy bridge, which is less about new theorems than about keeping the formalism honest: a soundness result relating the platform's commitment-and-entitlement tracking to Brandom's deontic scorekeeping (proving the commitment store does what inferentialist semantics says it should), and an operationalization of Negarestani's "equality of all minds" as a symmetry/anonymity axiom on the interaction, with the consequences of that axiom worked out — which is also what feeds the impossibility theorem in direction five.
