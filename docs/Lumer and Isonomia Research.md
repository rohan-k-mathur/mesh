Working through Christopher Lumer’s paper “A Theory of Philosophical Arguments”:

A) **Descriptive-nomological theories** identify invariant structures, regularities, and laws of human existence and the world. They use empirical-scientific methods and serve a "supply function" — providing factual premises needed by the other theory types. Distinguished from natural science by their orienting, self-enlightening intention rather than a purely technical one. (Hume's *Treatise*, Brandt's action theory, experimental moral psychology.)

**Idealizing-hermeneutic theories** aim at self-enlightenment about the practical sense of human practices, instruments, rules, and conceptual systems. They combine empirical understanding of existing practice with normative evaluation, filtering out the best elements and constructing an ideal instrument. The key move is identifying the *standard output* — what the instrument is really for. (Aristotle's *Organon*, Frege's *Begriffsschrift*, Goldman's reliabilism, Bayesian epistemology.)

**Technical-constructive theories** develop good, versatile instruments (logical systems, moral norms, argumentation rules, political institutions). They build on idealizing-hermeneutic results but are freer — not tied to already-existing structures. The starting point is specifying a desired standard output, then constructing and justifying a structure that optimally produces it. (Plato's *Republic*, Montesquieu's separation of powers, Gauthier's contractarian ethics, Beth's semantic tableaux.)

**Ontic-practical theories** address transcendental questions about realities beyond experience (external world, other minds, constancy of natural laws, theoretical entities, God). Since empirical proof is unavailable, these use Pascal-style practical arguments to show it is *epistemically optimal to behave as if* the thesis were true — a compromise that leaves the ontological question open but rationally vindicates our default practice. (Pascal's wager, Kant's practical postulates, Lumer's own arguments for the external world and regularity of nature.)

B) Argument Types: From Lumer's "Practical Theory of Argumentation" - three main groups: deductive (including descriptive-statistical), probabilistic (indicatory, genesis-of-knowledge, interpretive), and practical (prudential evaluation, instrument-justification, adequacy-condition arguments, welfare-ethical arguments, Pascal arguments). All are epistemological. They work by satisfying conditions of epistemological principles that guarantee thesis-acceptability.

C) Theses and arguments for each theory type: Each theory type is broken down into its characteristic thesis-types (definitions, axioms/structure descriptions, optimality judgments, etc.) and matched with the argument types suited to justify them. The key recurring finding: in every theory type, one of the central theses is an *optimality judgment* — that a given theory, instrument, or behavioral policy is the best available, and this requires practical arguments.

For descriptive-nomological theories, the central argumentative challenge is justifying the optimality of the axiom system (simplicity, scope, predictive power, etc.) via practical arguments for instruments, with subordinate deductive and probabilistic arguments for confirmation, coherence, and prediction.

For idealizing-hermeneutic and technical-constructive theories, the central challenge is justifying the claim that the proposed instrument is ideal, again via practical arguments, supported by interpretive arguments (to recover subjective reasons), nomological knowledge (from descriptive-nomological theories), and deductive/probabilistic arguments for functional claims.

For ontic-practical theories, the central challenge is the Pascal argument itself — showing that as-if behavior has the highest Laplacean expected desirability under conditions of total theoretical ignorance — supported by deductive arguments for the consistency of alternative possible worlds and the completeness of the alternative set.

 D) Self-application: The paper verifies that its own theory satisfies the thesis-types of an idealizing-hermeneutic theory (TIH1–TIH10): it defines its terms, describes its proposed structure, documents real examples from philosophy's history, explains function and functioning, identifies subjective and hypothetical reasons, determines a standard output, and argues for the optimality of its own framework.

The self-justification problem, taken seriously
Start by getting precise about what Lumer's grounding move actually is. The structure is: practical arguments justify instruments; the validity criteria for practical arguments are themselves instruments; those criteria are justified by practical arguments; regress threatens; Lumer halts it by anchoring in "the psychological structure of our decisions." The charitable reconstruction — which he doesn't spell out but which I think is the only viable reading — is constitutive instrumentalism: the criteria aren't derived from decision psychology as true conclusions from descriptive premises (that would be the is/ought violation he accuses naturalists of), but rather the normativity of the criteria is hypothetical-imperative normativity, and hypothetical imperatives need an anchor in actual motivational architecture to have grip. The terminus isn't a justified proposition; it's the fact that creatures with our decision structure cannot coherently ask for justification in any other currency. This is structurally Reichenbach's vindication strategy generalized: you don't prove the framework is correct, you show it's optimal relative to ends you actually have, and the demand for justification of those ends is either answerable in the same way or not a well-formed demand.
Whether the circle is then virtuous depends on a distinction worth making explicit: there's a difference between a framework that presupposes its own criteria when justifying them (vicious — any framework passes its own test) and one whose self-application is non-trivially satisfiable (it could have failed; the criteria could have turned out suboptimal by their own lights, forcing revision). Lumer's §10 self-application is only interesting if the second reading holds, and he doesn't demonstrate that it does. A worthwhile piece of conceptual work for your foundation: specify what it would look like for the metatheory to fail its own test. If you can't specify failure conditions, the self-justification is decorative.
Here's the consequence that matters for your software, and I'd treat it as a hard design commitment rather than a philosophical footnote: practical-argument validity is relativized to a desirability base. There is no shipping "this argument is valid" simpliciter for the practical arguments that, on Lumer's own account, sit at the center of every philosophical theory type. What a system can compute is validity-given-a-profile: a specified standard output, a set of desirability weightings, a consequence model. The honest architecture makes that relativization inspectable — the desirability base is a first-class, user-visible, contestable object, not a buried constant. This is also where Lumer's framework becomes genuinely self-applicable in a useful way: the system's own evaluation criteria are instruments with declared standard outputs, representable in the same schema the system uses for object-level arguments. A reflexive layer, in other words, where the metalevel is just more of the same data structure.
Two technical sub-problems then become tractable rather than merely troubling.
Partition-dependence. The Laplacean move in Pascal arguments (PP4, TOP4) inherits the classical objection to the principle of indifference: equiprobability over possible worlds depends on how you individuate worlds, different individuations yield different expected desirabilities, and permutation-construction (TOP3) guarantees completeness and exclusivity but not uniqueness of partition — you can permute at any granularity. Three responses worth developing, in ascending order of how much I'd bet on them. First, restrict admissible partitions by consequence-relevance: individuate states by their consequence profiles over the available acts, merging worlds that are decision-theoretically indistinguishable (this is essentially Savage's small-worlds discipline). That yields a canonical coarsest partition and removes most of the arbitrariness, though not all — coarsening can itself shift Laplacean ratios. Second, retreat from Laplace to imprecise probabilities with maximin or Hurwicz decision rules; the cost is that "optimal" weakens to "admissible," and Lumer's TOP5 theses become correspondingly weaker. Third — and this is the one I find most interesting because it's both a genuine refinement of Lumer and directly implementable — make partition-invariance a validity condition: the as-if thesis is justified only if it comes out optimal under every admissible partition. Computationally that's an enumeration-and-check over a partition lattice. Philosophically it converts a notorious objection into a strengthened criterion. If only one direction survives invariance testing (as plausibly happens for "behave as if the external world exists," where the alternative yields paralysis under any partition), Pascal arguments get more robust, not less.
The unprovable completeness condition. Lumer notes that practical arguments presuppose, without being able to prove, that all relevant consequences are covered. Epistemologically this is awkward; dialectically and computationally it's a solved problem-shape. Formalize practical arguments in a structured defeasible framework (ASPIC+-style, with Dung semantics underneath): the netting arithmetic runs on strict rules, while the completeness presupposition is an implicit defeasible assumption that functions as an attack point. The proponent never proves completeness; the opponent defeats by instantiating a missed relevant consequence, which triggers re-netting. Burden of proof is thereby allocated where it can actually be discharged. This does real conceptual work for Lumer's theory, not just engineering work: it explains why completeness needn't be proven — because the argument's epistemic force is constituted dialectically, by survival under attack, rather than demonstratively. It also explains his observation that practical arguments are non-monotonic in a way that integrates cleanly with thirty years of formal argumentation research, which his paper, somewhat oddly for an argumentation theorist, doesn't touch. I'd consider this bridge — Lumer's epistemological practical arguments rendered in Dung-style semantics with completeness as defeasible presupposition — the single most valuable piece of foundational work for your project, because it's where his theory and computational argumentation actually meet rather than coexist.

Conceptual engineering as the live counterpart of technical-constructive theory

The mapping is nearly one-to-one and worth writing out, because it imports an entire decade of objections and replies as free stress-testing for Lumer's TTC schema. Haslanger's ameliorative question ("what do we want the concept to do?") is TTC2, standard-output determination. The engineered concept is TTC3, the structure. Manipulability conditions and inferential role are TTC4/TTC5. And — the key point — the justification step that the conceptual engineering literature handles gesturally, Lumer supplies explicitly: TTC6/TTC7 practical arguments with full netting of advantages. Conceptual engineering has been criticized for lacking an account of when an engineering proposal is justified rather than merely advocated; Lumer's framework is that account, essentially ready-made.
The traffic runs the other way too, and this is where the conceptual refinement happens. Take the Strawsonian "changing the subject" objection: engineer the concept and you're no longer talking about knowledge or woman or cause, you're talking about something else. In Lumer's terms this objection dissolves into a tradeoff parameter, and I think the dissolution is correct: continuity with existing practice is an idealizing-hermeneutic constraint (TIH3's anchoring in factually realized structures), and the distance between IH and TC theories just is the degree of license to depart from found practice. Topic-continuity isn't an identity condition that engineering violates; it's one consequence-type in the netting — communication costs, coordination losses, uptake friction — with weight that varies by case. Sometimes the switching costs dominate (engineering "belief" away from folk usage would wreck more than it fixes); sometimes they're trivially outweighed (Haslanger's cases, arguably). The objection becomes a term in the evaluation function. Similarly Cappelen's implementation problem — we can't control meaning change, so engineering is idle — is, in decision-theoretic terms, just the requirement that consequence predictions include realistic uptake probabilities. A concept optimal-conditional-on-adoption but unadoptable loses to an inferior-but-adoptable rival on expected desirability. The literature treats this as a deep difficulty because it lacks the expected-value frame; with the frame, it's routine.
For the software, the payoff is a representation schema. The Walton tradition gives computational argumentation its scheme library, but Walton's practical-reasoning schemes are thin — goal, action, side-effect critical questions — compared to Lumer's full structure. A TTC/TIH-derived schema for normative and design arguments would represent any proposed instrument (concept, criterion, rule, institution, piece of your own system) as a tuple: standard output, structure description, function, way of functioning, alternatives set, consequence model, netting, with the completeness presupposition attached as the standing defeasible assumption from direction 1. Critical questions fall out of the thesis types: is the standard output itself justified (TTC6)? is the alternatives set adequate (the TIH10 premise-group about which structures enter the comparison)? are the consequence predictions sound? Each maps to an attack relation. And note the elegant closure: your system, being itself a technical-constructive artifact, can represent its own design rationale in its own schema — which is exactly the non-decorative self-application that direction 1 demanded.
One caution, since you're freed from institutional pressure but not from the obligation to keep the foundation honest: the two directions interlock at a point of genuine unresolved tension. The TTC schema requires a standard-output determination, and standard outputs are justified by practical arguments, whose validity is relativized to a desirability base (direction 1's conclusion). So the system's representation of any engineering argument bottoms out in a desirability profile someone supplied. Lumer would say the most basic criteria get justified by adequacy-condition arguments (his fallback when quantitative desirability isn't available), but adequacy conditions are themselves chosen — the regress reappears one level down, in qualitative dress. I don't think this is fatal; I think it means the foundation should explicitly embrace what it is — a framework for making instrumental justification transparent and attackable rather than for terminating it — and your software should treat "where does this evaluation bottom out, and who chose that" as a queryable property of every argument. That's both the philosophically defensible position and, conveniently, the more useful tool.

Having read the full isonomia overview document, the picture is much sharper than I expected, and the headline contact point is one I'd state bluntly: Isonomia's formal core is almost entirely alethic, while its highest-stakes outputs are practical. The evidence algebra folds support for truth (log-odds weight-of-evidence), ASPIC+ computes which arguments survive, Ludics computes interactive convergence — all of it machinery for "what is the case." But look at the application list: budget deliberations, planning processes, RFCs, architectural decisions, policy recommendations, program evaluations. Recommendation packets — the unit of institutional submission, the thing the hash-chained audit trail exists to dignify — are optimality judgments about instruments. That is exactly Lumer's territory: his central thesis is that the keystone claims of theorizing are value judgments justified by practical arguments (netting of consequences under a desirability base), and that this argument type is systematically under-formalized — present everywhere, "rudimentary, incomplete, not canonized in form, almost amorphous." Walton's practical-reasoning and argument-from-consequences schemes, which is what your catalog inherits, are thin instances of precisely the pattern he diagnoses. So Lumer isn't decoration for the foundation; he names the gap between what the engine evaluates and what the institutional layer ships. The two directions we developed now land at specific subsystems.
1. A practical-argument scheme family, with completeness as an assumption-typed premise. Your data model already has the slot: premises are typed ordinary/assumption/exception and may be flagged implicit. Lumer's full netting structure — value object, alternatives set, consequence model with probabilities, desirability weights, aggregation — becomes a scheme family where the unprovable completeness condition ("all relevant consequences are covered") is materialized as an implicit assumption-typed premise. That's the direction-1 dialectical resolution implemented in one move: the proponent never proves completeness; an opponent undermines the assumption premise by instantiating a missed consequence, which triggers re-netting. ASPIC+ handles assumption-attack natively, so the formal semantics is already paid for. The deeper payoff is for your critical-question machinery: Lumer's account is generative where Walton's is curated. Walton CQ lists are hand-annotated per scheme; on Lumer's account, critical questions for an epistemological argument family are derived from the validity conditions of its epistemological principle (for practical arguments: missed consequence, wrong consequence probability, contested desirability weight, inadequate alternatives set, plus partition-adequacy for the Pascal subfamily). This bears directly on your scheme-ontology trilemma result. You've shown a scheme is simultaneously behaviour, presentation, and protocol; Lumer supplies a fourth layer beneath all three — the epistemological principle — from which the other three are in principle derivable. That would upgrade "a scheme's identity is its critical questions" from a definition to a theorem: the CQs are the validity conditions, so schemes with the same conditions necessarily withstand the same questions. Your univalence formulation should survive this intact and arguably gets its missing why.
2. A desirability monoid in the evidence algebra, and culprit-sets over valuations. The closed-monoid registry currently holds log-odds, min, and product — all evidence-for-truth semantics. Practical conclusions don't fold that way; they fold as signed expected-desirability netting over a declared profile. Registering a fourth monoid family, parameterized by desirability base, is the concrete form of direction 1's relativization commitment: the system never computes "this recommendation is valid," it computes "valid given this profile," and the profile is a first-class auditable input — which is exactly your existing house style (your confidence cards already disclose every input, weight, and contribution; this extends that discipline from evidence weights to value weights). The culprit-set generalization is, I think, the single most valuable derived feature: alongside "what must I retract to reject this claim?" you get "which valuation must I not share, or which consequence claim must I deny, for this recommendation to fail?" — sensitivity analysis as deterministic graph query. For the deliberative-democracy applications this is arguably worth more than the alethic version, because it mechanically separates factual disagreement from value disagreement. And it composes with your minimal-disagreement extractor: the minimal unshared commitment between two parties gets typed — they diverge on a consequence prediction (resolvable by evidence) or on a desirability weight (resolvable, if at all, by a different kind of move). A facilitator cockpit that displays which kind of disagreement the room actually has is a genuinely new capability, and the four-theorem minimal-commitment arc is the substrate it would ride on.
3. Typed transport invariants for Plexus. Lumer's three argument families carry different relativization parameters, and these are monodromy sources your pseudofunctor result can be sharpened by. Deductive arguments are monotonic — they transport freely. Probabilistic arguments are valid relative to a database d — transporting one into a room with a different evidence base can silently invalidate it, so transport must either check database compatibility or downgrade standing. Practical arguments are valid relative to a desirability base — transporting a recommendation into a room with different declared values is a category error unless the argument is re-netted under the target's profile or the divergence is flagged. This gives your "transport is sound exactly when no claims drop or drift" theorem a semantic refinement: epistemological type predicts where drift lives. The one-hop contract already shows the right instinct; Lumer supplies the typing discipline that says which arguments needed the restriction and which never did.
4. Lumerian typing of the Opponent, and an honest tension with the interaction thesis. Here I should flag a real philosophical friction rather than smooth it over. Your organizing thesis is argumentation-as-interaction: validity is survival of every well-formed counter-interaction. Lumer is pointedly on the other side — his epistemological approach grounds validity in truth-conducive principles precisely because surviving interaction doesn't guarantee acceptability (an incompetent Opponent confers cheap orthogonality). The synthesis I'd defend, consistent with what we built in direction 1: the epistemological layer types the test space, the interactive layer computes survival within it. "Every well-formed counter-interaction" gets cashed out as: the Opponent designs against a practical argument are exactly those instantiating its validity-condition-derived attack types — missed consequence, contested weight, inadequate alternatives, bad partition. Lumer normatively constrains what the Opponent is entitled to test; Ludics evaluates the game. This raises a precise question for your keystone theorem: does the grounded ⇔ orthogonality correspondence remain strategy-preserving when the framework includes practical arguments whose standing rests on an attackable completeness assumption? My expectation is yes, since ASPIC+ assumption-attacks compile to ordinary attack edges, but the Proponent's design now includes a standing concession point (the completeness locus) that any Opponent can visit, and whether the bijection respects that locus structure is a checkable extension of the mechanized core. Relatedly, your scheme-rivalry conjecture has a ready-made historical corpus in Lumer: his Burkholder analysis — Pascal's wager pressed into modus ponens via the deductive minimum — is scheme-rivalry exactly as you define it (decision-theoretic and deductive schemes with disjoint behaviours over one claim, no Pollock primitive applying), and his interpretation conditions (clarity, authenticity, immanence, situational adequacy) are adjudication criteria for which rival classification wins. Your many-to-many scheme classification with per-scheme confidence is the detection surface; the literature's habit of deductivist over-reconstruction is labeled training data.
5. The deliberation dictionary is a conceptual-engineering surface, and direction 3 lands there almost without modification. Contested, versioned definitions challengeable through the dialogue protocol — that is ameliorative practice with infrastructure. Two imports. First, Lumer's sharpest small point: justifying a definition (pragmatic — formulability economy, memorability, capturing the structure in one word) is categorically distinct from justifying the structure the definition names, and conflating them is a diagnosable error. The protocol can enforce the distinction: a challenge filed against a dictionary entry is typed as either pragmatic-adequacy (attacks the terminological choice) or smuggled-thesis (actually attacks the underlying optimality claim, and gets redirected to the argument where that claim lives). Second, the Strawson-objection dissolution becomes operational: a proposed redefinition is a practical argument whose netting includes switching costs — uptake friction, coordination loss with prior usage, broken backlinks in the literal sense your graph makes vivid — as consequence-types with weights. "You're changing the subject" stops being a trump and becomes a quantified term the challenger must show dominates.
6. The reflexive layer, which your §VIII programme is already most of the way to. A room choosing logical vs. social vs. hybrid gating, a threshold, a monoid — these are desirability-base choices about what the room's evaluation optimizes for. Direction 1's conclusion was that these must be queryable, contestable objects rather than buried constants; your programme already holds itself to "the reasoning behind what ships is itself addressable, citable, contestable." The completion is to represent evaluation-parameter choices in the graph itself as TTC-schema arguments (standard output, alternatives considered, netting), so that "why does this room gate at 0.7 hybrid?" resolves to a permalink like any other claim. One caution from Lumer while you're there: the social scoring mode is, in his taxonomy, population intuitionism, and his objection stands — shared intuition without cognitive provenance is an indicator at best, never a justification. Your standing/score separation already respects this; the hybrid gate is the one place the two currencies mix, and the mixing weight is exactly the kind of choice the reflexive layer should expose.
