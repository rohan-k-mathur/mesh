Commonplace: the memory-infrastructure project 

--towards counterdegredation: the loss of durable personal memory under contemporary conditions of attention fragmentation, image oversupply, and the specific degradation of the note-keeping and diary traditions that allowed prior generations to maintain coherent relations to their own pasts.

infrastructure for personal memory that operates at the level of sustained life-work rather than at the level of productivity-app. Note-keeping systems like Obsidian, Notion, and the various Roam-adjacent tools address the problem partially but operate within specific assumptions (that the user wants efficient retrieval, that notes should be discoverable through search and link graphs, that the system's value is in the current state rather than in the accumulated state across time). The memory-infrastructure project would be different: it would take seriously the specific form of the long-term personal archive as a genre with its own architectural requirements.

The central claim the project would rest on is that serious personal memory-work is a practice — a specific discipline that shapes the subject who performs it — and that this practice requires infrastructure with specific architectural commitments that the current ecosystem does not provide. This is a philosophical claim before it is a technical one, and the philosophical claim matters because it determines what the software can and cannot be.

In Philosophy as a Way of Life and the earlier Exercices spirituels et philosophie antique, Hadot reconstructed the specific claim that ancient philosophy — particularly Stoic, Epicurean, and Platonic — was not primarily a body of theoretical propositions but a set of specific practices that shaped the practitioner. The practices included meditation on specific propositions, the examination of conscience, the specific discipline of maintaining a personal record of thought and observation, and the specific practices of attention that formed the backbone of ancient therapies of self. The textual artifacts we have from the ancients — Meditations, Letters from a Stoic, fragments of Epicurus, the Pythagorean Golden Verses — are not theoretical treatises but residues of specific practices. Marcus Aurelius's Meditations is not a book; it is the external record of a specific practice its author was engaged in, written for himself.

Foucault developed this in his late work. The specific concept of hypomnemata in his Care of the Self and in the late lectures identifies the practice of keeping notebooks — collections of readings, observations, self-examinations, and meditations on specific themes — as a central technology of the self in the ancient world. The hypomnemata was not a diary in the modern confessional sense; it was a working document for the subject's own formation, a space where the subject could constitute herself through specific practices of writing and rewriting.

The tradition did not end with antiquity. The commonplace book tradition extended through the medieval monastic practices, through the Renaissance humanist commonplacing (Erasmus, Montaigne, and the specific tradition of readers who structured their intellectual life around the sustained archive of excerpts, observations, and reflections), through the early-modern tradition of the philosophical notebook (Pascal's Pensées and the scattered notebooks of figures like Lichtenberg and Joubert), into the modern period (Emerson's journals, Nietzsche's notebooks, Kafka's diaries, Pessoa's extraordinary Book of Disquiet as the radical version, Benjamin's Arcades Project as the maximalist version, and the specific contemporary residues in figures like Sebald, Susan Sontag, and Annie Dillard).

What these practices share, across their specific differences, is a set of commitments that the current note-taking ecosystem does not take seriously. The commitments include: the archive is not a database but a site of sustained practice; the value of the archive is not in retrieval but in the specific formative work it makes possible; the subject's relation to her own archive changes across time, and the archive should support this changing relation rather than treating it as static; the archive is fundamentally private in ways that social infrastructure cannot accommodate; the practice of writing is itself the point, not a means to some other end.

Contemporary productivity culture has captured the surface of this tradition and hollowed it out. The "second brain" movement, the personal knowledge management ecosystem, the note-taking app industry — all of these treat the archive as a productivity tool, with the underlying assumption that the value of your notes is in what you can retrieve from them and how efficiently you can recombine them into outputs. The specific formative dimension is absent. The archive is a service to the self's productivity rather than a site of the self's formation.

The apparatus does not repress the practice — it optimizes it, converts it into a service-to-efficiency register, and thereby forecloses the specific practice the tradition was about. The note-taking-as-productivity-hack register is exactly the register that hollows out the tradition it claims to extend. The project's philosophical core would be the specific refusal of this register.

The specific architectural features, with more detail

Temporal depth as primary architectural principle. The current ecosystem treats notes as flat objects — they have timestamps but the timestamps are metadata, not structural. The archive's temporal dimension is treated as an attribute of individual notes rather than as a structural feature of the archive itself. The project would invert this: the temporal structure would be primary, and the archive would be represented in ways that make the long view of the archive's development visible and navigable.

Concretely: the interface would include specific modes for engaging with the archive across different temporal horizons. A one-week mode (for current work and recent thinking), a six-month mode (for the development of specific themes across a season of life), a multi-year mode (for the long view across the archive's life). Each mode would have specific affordances. The long-view mode would not be a search interface — it would be a specifically designed representation of how the archive has developed, which ideas have persisted, which have been abandoned, which have been transformed.

The specific inspiration here is the way a serious scholar's marginalia in his own working copies of texts develops across decades. The marginalia from 2015 is in specific conversation with the marginalia from 2025, and the archive's specific value emerges from the conversation between temporal layers. Current tools flatten this; the project would restore it.

The commonplace as genre rather than container. The commonplace book tradition distinguishes between specific genres of entry, and the distinction is not just organizational but formal. The excerpt (a passage from a read text, recorded for later meditation). The observation (something noticed in experience, recorded in its specific moment). The meditation (sustained thought on a specific theme, developed across multiple entries over time). The dialogue (imagined conversation, with oneself or with interlocutors real or historical). The letter unsent (written to specific addressees, as a specific discipline of clarification). The list (specific kinds of accumulating inventories that have their own formal discipline — Wittgenstein's numbered propositions, Pessoa's aphoristic fragments).

Current tools offer a single entry type (the note, flatly considered). The project would offer specific entry types with specific architectural affordances. An excerpt would be linked to its source text at the level of the citation graph, with specific commitments to preserving the source's own context. An observation would be marked as such, with specific attention to the moment of its recording. A meditation would be a specific formal object — a thread of entries, developed across time, on a specific named theme, with specific interface support for the return-to-the-theme practice that the genre requires. The entry types would not be tags (as they would be in current tools) but structurally different objects with different architectural treatments.

The implication is that the user would be trained, through the interface, into specific practices that the commonplace tradition supports. The interface would make specific practices more natural than others, not through coercion but through the specific ergonomic gradient the design establishes. The user who sits down to write would be prompted — not explicitly, but through interface affordances — to think about what kind of entry they are writing, and the different kinds would become specific disciplines the user develops across time.

Revision as first-class operation. The commonplace tradition treats revision as central. Marcus Aurelius returns to themes across years; Pessoa's fragments develop variations on the same themes; Wittgenstein's notebooks show specific propositions being reformulated across decades. The current note-taking ecosystem treats revision as either destructive (the note is updated in place, the old version is lost) or unwieldy (version histories exist but are not interface-primary).

The project would treat revision as architecturally central. Every entry would maintain its full revision history, not as a backup mechanism but as a visible dimension of the entry's being. A meditation on a theme, developed across five years of revisions, would be visible as a specific object whose revisions are themselves the work — you could read the meditation in any of its revision states, you could see the specific trajectory of development, you could annotate the trajectory itself. The archive is not a collection of current notes but a collection of specific developmental objects whose current state is one of many states in their ongoing life.

The interface implication is that the user's relation to her own earlier work is preserved rather than overwritten. The self who wrote five years ago is still in the archive, in conversation with the self who is writing now. The practice of re-engaging with earlier work is supported architecturally rather than left to the user's discipline.

The physical-digital bridge. Serious note-keeping does not happen exclusively in digital form, and the project would take this seriously. Many of the most productive practitioners in the commonplace tradition worked primarily or substantially in notebooks, and the specific phenomenology of handwriting differs from the specific phenomenology of typing in ways that matter for the practice. Margaret Nairn writes by hand, Ursula Le Guin wrote by hand, Cormac McCarthy composed in typewriter drafts — the specific physical practice shapes the thinking in ways the digital environment cannot replicate.

The project would support the translation between physical and digital without privileging either. This means specific tooling for capturing handwritten material into the archive (the current OCR tools are inadequate for the specific requirements of serious note-keeping; the specific problem is not reading the text but preserving the specific character of the handwritten original alongside the transcribed version). It means specific support for producing from the digital archive back into physical artifacts — beautifully-typeset printed volumes of specific meditations across years, bound in ways that make them usable as continuation of the notebook practice rather than as archived outputs.

The specific inspiration here is the medieval monastic practice of producing the specific formal artifacts (the beautiful manuscript, the bound volume) from the working archive. The monk's commonplace book and the produced manuscript were in specific relation — the working substrate and the formal artifact supported each other, and the practice depended on the specific back-and-forth between them. Contemporary tools have separated these; the project would restore the relation.

Privacy as architectural commitment. This is the feature that distinguishes the project most sharply from the current ecosystem. The current tools (Notion, Obsidian, Roam, the Apple Notes app) make various privacy claims but are all architected around cloud storage with specific dependencies on the provider's good behavior. Serious memory-work involves material that must be unconditionally private in ways the current infrastructure cannot guarantee.

The project's privacy commitments would be architecturally deep. Local-first storage with specific cryptographic guarantees. End-to-end encrypted sync across devices, with specific architecture that ensures the provider cannot read user data even in principle. No telemetry, no analytics, no network calls that touch user data. The specific design goal is that the user's archive is as private as a physical notebook locked in a drawer — with the specific advantages of digital infrastructure (search, cross-reference, backup) but without the specific vulnerability of cloud-hosted data.

This is technically demanding but not impossible. The specific infrastructure required (local-first software with cryptographic sync) exists in various incomplete forms across the industry, and the project would implement it to a standard higher than the current state of the art. The commitment to privacy-as-architectural-feature would differentiate the project from every commercial competitor and would ground the specific trust required for the serious practice.

The user experience across registers

The capture mode. You open the app. Cursor in a text field. Minimal chrome. You write. The writing might be a sentence, might be a page. When you are done, the interface prompts — subtly, not insistently — for what kind of entry this is. Excerpt, observation, meditation-on-a-theme, dialogue, letter, list. If it is a meditation on an existing theme, the interface surfaces the thread and shows the current state of the thread. If it is a new theme, the interface asks whether you want to name it or leave it unnamed for now. The capture is quick; the genre-assignment is quick; the result is that the entry enters the archive with its specific structural place already established.

The reading mode. You open the app with a specific theme in mind. The interface lets you enter the theme. The interface surfaces the specific thread of entries related to the theme, showing the development of the theme across time. You can read the thread as a connected whole, you can browse the specific entries, you can see the specific revisions that have shaped the current state of the thread. Reading the thread is itself a specific practice — you see how your thinking has developed, where it has stalled, where it has made unexpected connections to other threads.

The long-view mode. Once a month, or on a specific rhythm the user sets, the interface presents the long view of the archive's current state. Not a dashboard with metrics. A specific representation that shows which themes have been active, which have gone dormant, which have emerged recently. The user can engage with this long view as a specific practice of reflection on the archive itself — what have I been working on; what have I been avoiding; what connections have developed between themes that I have not explicitly noticed. This is the specific practice of the examination of conscience in the Ignatian tradition, rendered as a specific architectural feature.

The production mode. Occasionally, the practice produces something that wants to become a specific artifact — an essay, a letter, a published piece of writing. The interface supports the production of specific artifacts from the archive, with specific attention to the question of how the artifact's citation-and-development history is preserved. The essay that emerges from the archive carries, as metadata, its specific provenance in the archive — which entries fed into it, which revisions shaped it, which abandoned drafts preceded it. The artifact is a specific emergence from the archive rather than a separate object the archive provided raw material for.

The physical production mode. On a specific rhythm — yearly, perhaps — the user produces a physical artifact from a portion of the archive. A printed, bound volume of the year's meditations, beautifully typeset. The specific typography, binding, and design would be part of the project's commitment to the tradition's aesthetic. The artifact is both a record of the year's work and a specific object the user can return to in the physical register — pulled off a shelf, annotated by hand, carried on a trip. The digital archive and the physical volume are in specific relation.

Distinction from existing tools

The project would be specifically different from every existing tool in the space, and the differences are structural rather than cosmetic.

From Notion: Notion is a database with a text interface. The project is a practice with infrastructure. Notion's specific affordances are for collaborative productive work; the project's affordances are for solitary formative work. Notion assumes its users want to build workflows; the project assumes its users want to develop themselves.

From Obsidian: Obsidian is the closest existing tool to the specific vision, and the project would draw heavily on what Obsidian has gotten right. The specific differences are that Obsidian is architected around the knowledge graph (the links between notes are primary) where the project is architected around the temporal structure (the development across time is primary); Obsidian treats all notes as equivalent where the project recognizes specific genres with specific affordances; Obsidian has no specific stance on the philosophical tradition of commonplacing where the project is grounded in that tradition.

From Roam: Roam's specific emphasis on bidirectional linking and daily notes is aligned with some of the project's commitments. But Roam's specific aesthetics and specific theoretical commitments (networked thought, Tools for Thought) are different from the project's. Roam treats the graph as the primary object; the project treats the practice as the primary object, with the graph as one of several structural features that support the practice.

From Apple Notes, Google Keep, Evernote: These are productivity tools with no specific philosophical stance beyond efficiency. The project is categorically different.

From the various "second brain" productivity systems (Tiago Forte, the PARA method, and so on): These are methodologies with software support. The project is a specific philosophical-architectural commitment that makes the methodology unnecessary by building the practice into the infrastructure. Users of the project would not need to learn a methodology; the infrastructure would train them into the practice through ergonomic gradient.

From the various scholarly tools (Zotero, Tropy, DEVONthink): These are specialized tools for specific scholarly workflows. The project is general-purpose for serious personal memory-work, with specific features that would support scholarly use but without privileging it.

The specific questions the project would need to answer
Several open questions that the project would need to resolve through its specific design choices.
The onboarding question. The specific practice the project supports is unfamiliar to most contemporary users. How do you onboard someone who has never encountered the commonplace tradition without either overwhelming them with theory or reducing the project to a more conventional note-taking app? The answer probably involves specific onboarding material — not tutorials but specific invitations into the practice, perhaps in the form of excerpts from the tradition itself, specific prompts that let the user begin the practice before they fully understand what they are doing.
The scope-of-archive question. How large does an archive become across ten or twenty years of serious practice? The technical infrastructure must handle archives that may reach millions of entries across a lifetime. Current tools struggle at much smaller scales. The specific design must ensure that the archive remains usable as it grows, which probably means architectural choices about indexing, caching, and the specific ways the interface handles scale that the current generation of tools has not solved.
The genre-drift question. The commonplace tradition's specific genres may not all be relevant to contemporary users, and new genres may be needed that the tradition did not anticipate. The project must be specific enough in its genre definitions to train users into specific practices but flexible enough to let the genres develop with the users' practices. This probably means the genres are proposed defaults that users can customize and extend, with the defaults grounded in the tradition but the customization grounded in each user's specific practice.
The community question. The commonplace tradition is fundamentally solitary, and the project commits to this. But serious practitioners benefit from specific forms of community — shared reading, discussion of the practice, specific mentoring relationships. The project would need to support these without becoming a social platform. The specific answer probably involves very minimal, carefully-architected features for sharing specific artifacts between users who explicitly choose to share — not the default social-platform approach of opting out, but the specific practice of deliberate sharing of specific pieces in specific contexts. The tradition's precedent is the letter, which is shared writing between specific correspondents. The project might support letter-writing as a specific genre with specific features for exchange with named addressees.
The monetization question. The project is philosophically opposed to the specific monetization patterns of the current ecosystem (attention capture, data extraction, subscription-as-entrapment). It would probably be sustained through a combination of one-time software purchase (like the older commercial software tradition), specific commissioned work (the physical artifacts could be produced for specific fees that sustain the broader project), and possibly a small community of serious users who sustain the project through voluntary commitment. The commitment to not-maximizing-revenue is itself philosophical and would shape what the project can and cannot be.
The longevity question. The archive must be readable across decades, which means the specific file formats and architectural choices must prioritize durability over specific technical advantages. Plain text, open formats, specific commitments to data portability, and the specific ability to export the entire archive in forms that remain readable independent of the specific software. The user must be able to abandon the software at any time without losing the archive, and the software must be architected to make this guarantee credible.
What the project is not
It is not a productivity tool. It does not optimize for efficiency or output. It may, as a side effect, support specific forms of intellectual production, but the production is incidental rather than primary.
It is not a therapy tool. It is philosophically adjacent to contemplative traditions with therapeutic effects, but it does not claim to provide therapy, and it specifically refuses the specific register of the therapeutic apparatus that the Anti-Sex Framework critiques.
It is not a social platform. The archive is fundamentally private. Specific features for sharing with specific others exist but are architecturally secondary.
It is not a knowledge-management tool in the technical sense. It is not organized around the extraction of knowledge from the archive for use in other projects. Extraction is possible but is incidental rather than central.
It is not an AI assistant. The project would be skeptical of the specific use of AI in the contemplative context, because the specific value of the practice is in the specific human formation that the AI substitute would undermine. The project might use AI for specific technical purposes (OCR of handwritten material, search assistance for very large archives), but would not integrate AI into the contemplative practice itself.

What Commonplace would specifically need

The central data model would be an entry as atomic unit, with specific genres (excerpt, observation, meditation, dialogue, letter, list) as typed specializations. This maps cleanly onto the claim-as-atomic-unit model with some adaptations. An "entry" in Commonplace is structurally similar to a "claim" in Isonomia — a first-class object with metadata, provenance, revision history, and specific relations to other entries. The specific genres would be implemented as a typology of entry subtypes, which is how Walton schemes are implemented in Isonomia (a typology of argument subtypes). The infrastructure for handling typed atomic units with genre-specific behavior is directly transferable.

The temporal depth requirement is not currently central to Isonomia but is not architecturally absent. Commitment stores track speech-acts across time; revision histories preserve development across versions. What Commonplace needs additionally is specific interface affordances for engaging with the temporal structure at different horizons (one-week view, six-month view, multi-year view). These are interface-layer features built on data already present in the foundational model. The infrastructure supports this; the specific views would need to be built.

The thread concept — a meditation developed across entries over time — maps onto something like Isonomia's cumulative deliberation feature, where second-round processes build on first-round reasoning. The architectural primitive is the same: a named structure that accumulates contributions across time with specific continuity. In Isonomia this is applied to deliberations; in Commonplace it would be applied to meditation-threads. Same infrastructure, different domain application.

The citation graph that preserves source-text context for excerpts maps onto Isonomia's existing citation and evidence infrastructure. A claim in Isonomia can be linked to its source, with the source's specific context preserved; an excerpt in Commonplace needs the same treatment. This is already built.

The revision-as-first-class-object commitment is architecturally central to both projects. Isonomia's existing infrastructure for tracking revision histories of claims and arguments would transfer directly to revision histories of entries. This is a significant infrastructural win — the specific data structures for maintaining rich revision histories with navigable state are hard to build well and already exist in Isonomia.

The Plexus graph-of-graphs architecture is interestingly applicable. A Commonplace user's archive is not a single graph but a graph-of-graphs — the graph of entries within a theme, connected to other themes through specific meta-edges (shared excerpts, shared sources, cross-references between meditations). The Plexus formalism, with its typed meta-edges, maps directly onto this. The meta-edge types would be different (not "transported arguments" but "shared source," "thematic resonance," "developmental predecessor"), but the architectural primitive is the same.
