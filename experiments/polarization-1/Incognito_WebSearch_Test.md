Now I have sufficient evidence to write the synthesis. Here it is:

1. Verdict: CONTESTED (with momentum toward support)
The central claim — that algorithmic content curation has been a significant causal factor in US affective-polarization growth from 2012 to 2024, where "significant" means ≥10% of observed growth — cannot be confidently affirmed or rejected on the current public record. The strongest experimental evidence cuts in opposite directions depending on platform, time period, and what dimension of "the algorithm" is being manipulated. However, the evidentiary trajectory over the last two years has shifted meaningfully against the skeptical position, thanks to independent experimental work on X that produced causal estimates consistent with practical significance, and to methodological critiques that have undermined the internal validity of the most-cited null-result studies. The honest summary is that the literature is converging on "algorithms probably matter, but we still cannot confidently bound the magnitude at the 12-year, population level," which means the ≥10% threshold remains genuinely contested rather than clearly met or clearly missed.

2. Three Load-Bearing Pieces of Evidence on Each Side
Evidence supporting the claim
First, the 2024 X/Twitter feed-reranking experiment, published in Science in late 2025, is the strongest single piece of causal evidence in the literature. A team led by researchers at multiple universities built a platform-independent browser extension that used an LLM to rerank participants' X feeds in real time, upweighting or downweighting content expressing antidemocratic attitudes and partisan animosity (AAPA). Over a 10-day preregistered field experiment with 1,256 participants during the 2024 presidential campaign, shifting AAPA exposure moved out-party partisan animosity by more than two points on a 100-point feeling thermometer. The authors and independent commentators noted that this effect size is comparable to several years of background polarization drift in long-running ANES panel data, and it was achieved through content reranking alone — the same posts, reordered. arXiv
Second, the Allcott et al. (2020, American Economic Review) Facebook deactivation experiment found that deactivating Facebook for four weeks before the 2018 US midterm election reduced political polarization alongside reducing news knowledge and increasing subjective well-being. This remains the cleanest whole-platform-removal experiment showing a polarization effect, though the effect bundled algorithmic curation with all other Facebook exposure channels. American Economic Association
Third, the Lorenz-Spreen et al. (2023, Nature Human Behaviour) systematic review of 496 articles found that detrimental associations between digital media and democracy were more commonly observed in established democracies, with polarization among the outcomes showing harmful associations. A 2025 replication confirmed the original findings with updated data through March 2024. Nature
Evidence against the claim
First, the Guess et al. (2023, Science) suite of three papers from the US 2020 Facebook and Instagram Election Study constitutes the highest-powered experimental evidence on the skeptical side. The chronological feed led to users' spending less time on the site but did not substantially change their attitudes about politics. Switching from algorithmic to chronological ranking, or reducing like-minded content, produced no measurable shift in affective polarization, issue polarization, or political knowledge over periods of weeks to months. Science
Second, the Allcott et al. (2024, PNAS) expanded deactivation experiment for the 2020 election found that the effects of both Facebook and Instagram deactivation on affective and issue polarization were all precisely estimated and close to zero — a striking reversal from the same team's 2018 finding. This null result, across nearly 35,000 participants deactivated for six weeks during a presidential campaign, is difficult to dismiss on power grounds. PNAS
Third, the Boxell, Gentzkow, and Shapiro (2017, PNAS) demographic analysis found that polarization has increased the most among the demographic groups least likely to use the Internet and social media — adults over 75 showed larger increases on eight of nine polarization measures than adults 18–39. This remains the most widely cited macro-level evidence against a causal internet-to-polarization pathway, though it predates the period of heaviest algorithmic optimization and does not isolate algorithmic curation from internet use generally. PNAS

3. The Single Development That Has Most Weakened One Side's Case
The skeptical side's case has been most damaged by the "break-glass" critique of the Meta 2020 election studies. In 2024, Bagchi, Grabowicz, and colleagues (published in a Science response and related work) demonstrated that Meta introduced 63 "break glass" changes to Facebook's news feed, which were expressly designed to diminish the visibility of untrustworthy news surrounding the 2020 U.S. presidential election. These changes overlapped with the experimental period of the Guess et al. studies but were not reported in the original papers. The critique showed that Meta's own algorithm was already significantly altered in the direction the experiment was testing, meaning the "control" condition (the standard algorithm) was already operating in a suppressed-inflammatory-content mode. This contamination makes it difficult to interpret the null results as evidence that algorithmic curation, under normal operating conditions, has no effect on polarization. Andy Guess, the lead author, acknowledges the finding might not apply to other platforms or conditions. Combined with the independent X reranking experiment that found significant effects using a method requiring no platform cooperation, this development has substantially eroded the strongest empirical pillar of the skeptical position. UMass AmherstScience

4. The Strongest Unresolved Empirical Challenge
The study that would most shift the verdict — in either direction — is a long-duration, multi-platform experiment measuring general-equilibrium effects on content production, not just individual-level exposure effects. Every existing experiment, including the X reranking study, manipulates what users see in the short term (days to weeks) while holding constant what other users create. But as the PNAS Nexus paper by Bagchi et al. (2025) explicitly noted, the scope of Guess et al.'s research does not encompass "general equilibrium" effects, such as the possibility for ranking algorithms to indirectly shape user attitudes by incentivizing and changing the type of content that users produce in the first place. If engagement-maximizing algorithms systematically reward partisan animosity in production — selecting for increasingly hostile content over months and years — then short-run exposure experiments will structurally underestimate the total algorithmic contribution to polarization. A study that randomized algorithmic incentives at the creator level for six or more months, measuring downstream shifts in content tone and audience attitudes, would directly test whether the current literature is measuring the right thing. If such a study found large production-side effects, the claim would be strongly supported; if it found null production effects, the skeptical position would be substantially reinforced. Oxford Academic

5. Two Areas Where the Public Debate Is Structurally Underdeveloped
First: cross-platform cumulative exposure. Nearly all experimental evidence studies a single platform in isolation. The average American in this period used multiple algorithmically curated platforms simultaneously (Facebook, Instagram, YouTube, X, TikTok, Reddit). No study has attempted to estimate the joint or interactive effect of multi-platform algorithmic curation on polarization. The Boxell et al. cross-national work (2022, Review of Economics and Statistics) showed that the United States experienced the sharpest rise in affective polarization among 12 OECD countries, with several countries experiencing decreases despite comparable social media penetration. This cross-national variation is often cited by skeptics, but it could equally reflect differences in platform mix, algorithmic tuning for the US market, or the interaction of algorithms with a two-party system. No study has tried to decompose the US-specific polarization trajectory into platform-specific algorithmic contributions, and no cross-national study has controlled for differences in algorithmic configuration across markets. ResearchGate
Second: the 2012–2016 transition period. The claim's timeframe begins in 2012, but the heaviest shift toward engagement-maximizing algorithmic feeds occurred between roughly 2013 (Facebook's News Feed ranking overhaul) and 2016 (YouTube's recommendation engine shift). This is also the period of the largest jump in measured affective polarization. No study has attempted a difference-in-differences or instrumental-variable design exploiting the staggered rollout of algorithmic feeds across platforms or user segments during this critical window. The Boxell et al. (2017) demographic analysis covers 1996–2016 but treats "internet use" as a monolithic variable, making no attempt to separate pre-algorithmic internet use from the specific introduction of engagement-ranked feeds. This is the most consequential missing empirical wedge in the entire debate: the claim is fundamentally about a technology that was deployed at scale in a specific window, and no one has tried to exploit the deployment timing for identification.
