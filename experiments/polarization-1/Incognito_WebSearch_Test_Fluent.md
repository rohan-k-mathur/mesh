## Algorithmic Curation and American Polarization: Where the Debate Actually Stands

The claim that social media algorithms have significantly driven U.S. affective polarization since 2012 sits at the intersection of at least four distinct disputes — definitional, empirical, methodological, and structural — and collapsing them into a single yes-or-no question is itself part of the problem. Here is what each layer involves and what the evidence actually says.

### What's being disputed, and why it matters

The first disagreement is definitional. "Algorithmic content curation" can mean the engagement-maximizing ranking function that decides what you see, but it can also encompass the broader incentive structure that shapes what gets *produced* in the first place. Most experimental work tests only the first sense — swapping one ranking for another — while the claim people care about often implicates the second. This gap is not pedantic; it determines whether null results from feed-swap experiments should be read as exonerating algorithms or as failing to measure the right thing.

The second is empirical: how large are the measured effects? The third is methodological: can short-duration experiments on consenting volunteers tell us anything about cumulative, population-level attitude change over a decade? And the fourth is structural: even if algorithms contribute, is their contribution large enough relative to partisan media ecosystems, elite polarization, and demographic sorting to clear a 10% threshold?

### The strongest case for skepticism

The most credible skeptical evidence came from the 2023 Facebook and Instagram Election Studies (FIES), a suite of experiments published in *Science* and *Nature*. Guess et al. (2023, *Science*) randomly assigned roughly 20,000 users to chronological feeds instead of Facebook's default algorithmic ranking for several months spanning the 2020 U.S. election. The chronological feed substantially changed *what people saw* — more political content, more untrustworthy sources — but produced no detectable shifts in affective polarization, issue polarization, or political knowledge. A companion study by Nyhan et al. (2023, *Nature*) found that exposure to like-minded sources on Facebook was prevalent but similarly not polarizing in itself. These were massive, preregistered, platform-partnered experiments — the kind the field had been begging for.

Supporting this picture, Boxell, Gentzkow, and Shapiro (2017, *PNAS*) showed that between 1996 and 2016, polarization grew fastest among Americans over 65 — the demographic least likely to use social media — and slowest among 18-to-39-year-olds. If social media were the primary engine, you would expect the opposite gradient. A 2025 study by Liu et al. (*PNAS*) found that short-term exposure to YouTube's recommendation system had limited effects on ideological polarization. And Altay, Hoes, and Wojcieszak (2025, *Nature Human Behaviour*) found that following news organizations on Instagram and WhatsApp boosted factual knowledge and trust with no significant effect on affective polarization.

Taken together, the skeptical case says: the best-powered experiments testing the most obvious causal channel — algorithmic ranking versus chronological — find that the channel doesn't move political attitudes much, and the demographic pattern of polarization growth doesn't fit an internet-centric story.

### The strongest case that algorithms matter

The case on the other side has strengthened considerably since late 2025, and it rests on a crucial methodological insight: comparing an algorithmic feed to a chronological one is the *wrong counterfactual*. Chronological feeds on politically charged platforms are themselves saturated with partisan hostility; the question isn't "algorithm versus no algorithm" but "what specific content does the algorithm amplify?"

Jia et al. (2025, *Science*) built a browser extension that used a large language model to rerank posts on X (formerly Twitter) during the 2024 presidential campaign, specifically targeting content expressing antidemocratic attitudes and partisan animosity (AAPA). In a 10-day experiment with 1,256 participants, reducing exposure to AAPA content shifted out-party feeling thermometer ratings by about 2 points on a 100-point scale; increasing exposure produced a symmetric cooling of roughly 2.5 points. The intervention didn't remove any content — it merely changed the ordering. And 74% of participants reported noticing no change to their experience.

The Prosocial Ranking Challenge (Stray et al., 2026 preprint), the largest independent multi-platform experiment to date, assigned 9,386 users to one of five alternative prosocial ranking algorithms across Facebook, Reddit, and X for six months during the 2024 election. The pooled treatment reduced affective polarization by 0.03 standard deviations, including a 1.5-degree decrease on feeling thermometers — small per-person but statistically significant and sustained over a long period.

These findings converge on a revised mechanistic story. The problem isn't filter bubbles or ideological segregation per se — the original "echo chamber" hypothesis has been substantially weakened. Rather, engagement-optimizing algorithms disproportionately surface content laden with outrage and partisan hostility, because such content drives clicks, shares, and time-on-platform. Over years and at population scale, this amplification of hostility can compound into meaningful shifts in affective polarization even if any single user's attitude shift is small.

### What has been weakened, and what survives

Over the last three years, two pillars of the debate have been significantly undermined.

First, the original echo-chamber hypothesis — the idea that algorithms trap people in homogeneous information silos — is largely dead as a direct explanation for polarization. The FIES studies, alongside earlier work by Bakshy, Messing, and Adamic (2015), showed that most users encounter plenty of cross-cutting content. Ideological segregation on social media is real but modest compared to, say, cable news consumption.

Second, the FIES null results, which were initially treated as near-definitive, have been seriously complicated. Bagchi, Menczer, and colleagues (2024, *Science*) documented that Meta implemented 63 "break-glass" emergency measures to its news feed algorithm during the study period — changes designed to suppress inflammatory and untrustworthy content in the weeks surrounding the 2020 election. These measures were not reported in the original Guess et al. paper. The algorithmic feed that participants were being compared *to* was therefore not Facebook's normal operating algorithm but a temporarily sanitized version of it. This doesn't invalidate the FIES results, but it substantially narrows their external validity: they may tell us that a chronological feed isn't better than an *already-moderated* algorithmic feed, not that Facebook's standard algorithm is benign.

What survives is the core finding that content *type* matters more than content *source*. Jia et al.'s key innovation was targeting the specific content dimension — partisan animosity and antidemocratic attitudes — that theory predicts should be most consequential. Previous experiments that merely shuffled the ranking order or changed exposure to cross-partisan content were testing the wrong lever. This reframing reconciles the apparently contradictory results: algorithms matter not because they create ideological echo chambers, but because they amplify the specific kinds of hostile content that shift affective attitudes.

### The development that would most change the verdict

The single most consequential piece of missing evidence is a long-duration, population-representative study of what happens when engagement-optimizing algorithms are persistently modified to downrank partisan hostility — not for 10 days, not for 6 months with opt-in volunteers, but at actual platform scale over years. Every existing experiment faces the same limitation: short treatment windows applied to self-selected samples, measured against a backdrop where the broader information ecosystem remains unchanged. The Jia et al. 2-point thermometer shift from a 10-day intervention is suggestive, but extrapolating it to a decade requires assumptions about cumulative compounding, feedback effects on content production, and spillovers to non-treated users that no current study can validate. If someone ran a genuine multi-year platform-level intervention and found, say, that it reduced the decadal growth in affective polarization by 15%, the debate would be largely over. If they found it reduced it by 2%, the skeptics would be vindicated. We do not currently have this evidence, and platforms have no incentive to generate it.

### What isn't being studied that should be

The most structurally underdeveloped area is the *supply-side* effect of algorithms on content production. Nearly all empirical work treats the content ecosystem as fixed and asks how different ranking functions change exposure. But algorithms don't just sort existing content — they shape what gets *created*. If engagement optimization rewards outrage, then over time the pool of available content tilts toward hostility regardless of how any individual's feed is ranked. Knight Columbia's work (2024) flags this explicitly: the FIES studies cannot capture "general equilibrium" effects in which ranking algorithms change the incentives for content production. Theoretical models (e.g., recent work in the *Journal of Public Economics*, 2026) confirm that supply-side effects can dominate exposure-side effects, but empirical measurement of this channel at scale remains essentially absent. This is the debate's largest blind spot.

### Verdict

The evidence supports a directional judgment: algorithmic content curation on major U.S. social media platforms has been a *contributory* causal factor in the growth of affective polarization between 2012 and 2024, operating primarily through the amplification of partisan hostility rather than through ideological segregation. The Jia et al. and Prosocial Ranking Challenge results provide genuine causal evidence of this mechanism, and the break-glass critique substantially weakens the FIES null results that previously anchored the skeptical position.

Whether the contribution clears the specific 10% threshold is harder to say, and I think the honest answer is that we don't know with confidence. The existing experimental effect sizes are individually small, but they are measured over short durations and do not capture supply-side feedback loops or cumulative compounding. The Boxell-Gentzkow-Shapiro demographic pattern remains a genuine puzzle — polarization did grow substantially among low-internet-use demographics, which means other forces (partisan cable news, elite polarization, geographic sorting) are doing significant work too. Algorithms are likely one cause among several, not the dominant one.

My overall assessment: the claim is *probably true* at the 10% threshold, but the confidence interval is wide enough that it could plausibly be false. I'd put maybe 60–65% probability on algorithms contributing at least 10% of observed affective-polarization growth, with the remaining uncertainty driven almost entirely by the absence of long-duration, supply-side, and population-scale evidence. The direction of the effect is no longer seriously in doubt; the magnitude is.
