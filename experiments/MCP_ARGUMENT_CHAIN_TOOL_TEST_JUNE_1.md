The proposal
EU Member States should pay landholders a results-based, per-hectare-year payment indexed to the measured annual mean water table on drained organic (peat) soils — money flows for keeping the table in the shallow band (roughly 0 to −30 cm) where peat oxidation is suppressed — financed by reallocating CAP area-based direct payments that currently subsidise those same soils in their drained state, and paired with paludiculture support so the land stays in production rather than being abandoned.

The design choices aren't incidental; each answers a specific failure mode. Pay for the water table, not for practices, because the water table is the proximate physical driver of emissions and is cheaply and continuously measurable — that solves monitoring and additionality. Reallocate rather than spend new money, because the status quo is a perverse subsidy: the EU currently pays per hectare to keep emitting land in cultivation. And bolt on paludiculture because the proposal's biggest weakness (below) is displacement, and keeping the land productive shrinks the displaced quantity.
