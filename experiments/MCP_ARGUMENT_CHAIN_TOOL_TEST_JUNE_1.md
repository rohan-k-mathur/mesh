The proposal
EU Member States should pay landholders a results-based, per-hectare-year payment indexed to the measured annual mean water table on drained organic (peat) soils — money flows for keeping the table in the shallow band (roughly 0 to −30 cm) where peat oxidation is suppressed — financed by reallocating CAP area-based direct payments that currently subsidise those same soils in their drained state, and paired with paludiculture support so the land stays in production rather than being abandoned.

The design choices aren't incidental; each answers a specific failure mode. Pay for the water table, not for practices, because the water table is the proximate physical driver of emissions and is cheaply and continuously measurable — that solves monitoring and additionality. Reallocate rather than spend new money, because the status quo is a perverse subsidy: the EU currently pays per hectare to keep emitting land in cultivation. And bolt on paludiculture because the proposal's biggest weakness (below) is displacement, and keeping the land productive shrinks the displaced quantity.

Walking through the structure
Which strands converge. One keystone node sits at the root: draining peat admits oxygen → microbial oxidation → CO₂/N₂O, at a rate rising with water-table depth (Evans et al. 2021, Nature 593:548–552 — water table is the "overriding control"). That node branches — it feeds both the emissions-magnitude strand and the cost-effectiveness strand. That branch is the non-serial bit you flagged: one premise doing double duty downstream.

From there, five strands converge on the proposal node:

Magnitude/leverage — ~0.3% of global land, ~1.9 Gt CO₂e/yr, ~5% of anthropogenic emissions; ~2% of EU farmland but ~25% of EU agricultural emissions (Leifeld & Menichetti 2018, Nat. Commun. 9:1071; IUCN; Tanneberger et al. 2022).
Cost-effectiveness — rewetting cuts on-site CO₂ sharply and is ~3.4× less nitrogen-costly and far less land-hungry than mineral-soil carbon sequestration (Leifeld & Menichetti 2018, Abstract).
Robust co-benefits — biodiversity, water quality, subsidence, fire, flood; this strand is deliberately structured so the case survives even if you discount carbon heavily (FAO).
Design rationale — emissions track the water table on a sigmoid, so target the shallow band; emissions are mappable and concentrated (Koch et al. 2023, Biogeosciences 20:2387, Fig. 7; the 2025 EU hotspot map).
Funding/feasibility — CAP pays for the drained state; the 2024 Nature Restoration Regulation already sets peatland targets (30/40/50%) but leaves rewetting voluntary and unfunded (EUR-Lex; European Parliament).
Where the objection lands. I wired two objections at different targets, which is the distinction you asked me to be precise about:

The steelman is displacement / indirect land-use change, and it's an inference attack (UNDERCUTS), not a premise attack. It concedes the mechanism, the magnitude, and the on-site cut — and denies that per-hectare savings aggregate to a net global benefit, because food production pushed off rewetted peat can reappear as cropland expansion elsewhere, regenerating the emissions (Evans et al. 2020). The EU conceded this risk itself by writing a food-security "emergency brake" into the law. In the graph it sits as an UNDERCUTS edge onto the cost-effectiveness node — it severs the warrant carrying that node into the conclusion, rather than denying the node.

The methane objection is a premise attack (UNDERMINES) on the same cost-effectiveness node: rewetting re-establishes CH₄, so the net benefit is smaller than the CO₂ saving alone. It lowers a magnitude rather than cutting an inference. I attached its own limiting condition as an exception premise: Günther et al. 2020 finds rewetting still yields an immediate net radiative-forcing benefit despite methane — so this dents the magnitude without overturning it.

Asserted vs. supposed. Everything in the five converging strands is asserted as established: the oxidation mechanism, the land area, the ~1.9 Gt figure (with its stated 0.31–3.38 range), the relative cost-effectiveness, the co-benefits, the water-table–emissions coupling, and the policy facts (CAP, the NRR targets). Two claims are walled off in scopes and assert nothing about the actual world: the counterfactual "halving drainage depths → ~1% of all anthropogenic emissions" (Evans et al. 2021), and the hypothetical "if adopted and complied with, emissions fall and benefits (~€1.86tn) exceed costs (~€154bn)" (Carbon Brief, on the Commission's impact assessment). Those are the "if X, then Y" steps; I gave them no support edges into the asserted proposal precisely so they can't leak.

The weakest link — and I'd put real money on this being the one a serious critic breaks first. It is not the mechanism (settled) and not the magnitude (well-replicated). It's the step from large on-site / per-hectare savings → large net global benefit, once you net out both leakage and methane. That single inferential joint is where both objections land, and it's where the evidence is genuinely thinnest, because leakage coefficients are commodity- and region-specific and poorly constrained. The honest tension in my own design: the displacement objection bites hardest exactly on high-yield cropland — which is also where rewetting saves the most emissions — and least on low-productivity drained grassland. So the hotspot-targeting and paludiculture features blunt the objection but don't dissolve it; on the most valuable parcels they trade some abatement for less leakage. I'd assert the proposal confidently for low-output drained fen grassland (much of the EU fen area) and only conditionally for intensive horticultural peat.
